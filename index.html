common questions

Questions people actually ask before starting

Straight answers — no scare tactics, no upsell. If you've got a question that isn't here, the EPA's own FAQ is authoritative.

How much does the real exam cost?

Typically $20–$50 per section, depending on the proctor. Universal (all four sections) usually runs $60–$150 total. ESCO, HVAC Excellence, and RSES sit on the lower end; some community colleges charge more to cover proctoring overhead. Type I through Mainstream Engineering is completely free online if that's all you need.

How long does it take to prepare?

Most people pass with 10–20 hours of focused study spread over one or two weeks. If you already work in the trade, less. If you're brand new to refrigeration, plan closer to 25 hours. The test rewards memorizing specific numbers (80% fill, 500 µ, 25 mm Hg, etc.) more than deep reasoning, so flashcards and the cheat sheet pull a lot of weight.

Can I really take it online from home?

Type I only can be open-book online — Mainstream Engineering offers this free. Type II, Type III, and any closed-book Core must be proctored, either in person at an approved test center or via a remote proctoring service that watches you on webcam. Anyone promising "Universal certification online with no proctor" is selling a scam — the EPA doesn't recognize it.

What happens if I fail a section?

You just retake that section. Passed sections carry over — you don't start from zero. Retake fees are usually the same as the original ($15–$40), and most proctors let you retake the same day or the next business day. No waiting period mandated by the EPA.

Do I need Type II or Universal? How do I decide?

If you only ever work on residential and light-commercial A/C and heat pumps, Core + Type II is enough. If you touch low-pressure centrifugal chillers (big commercial buildings, hospitals, data centers), add Type III. Since the Type III exam is the same price and same format, most techs just take all four and get Universal — the difference in effort is small and the cert lasts forever.

Does Section 608 ever expire?

No. Once you pass, you're certified for life. No renewal, no continuing education, no retest. Just keep your card (or a photo of it) accessible — you'll need to show it to buy refrigerant in containers of 2 lb or more.

Is there a minimum age to take the 608?

The EPA sets no minimum age. High-school vocational students regularly pass 608 as part of their HVAC program. A few individual proctors set their own minimums (usually 16 or 18) — call ahead if that's a concern.

Will my state accept this cert?

Yes — Section 608 is a federal certification and is valid in every U.S. state. A few states (Wisconsin, New York City, California for full HVAC contractor work) require additional state-level licensing on top, but those don't replace 608. 608 is always the foundation.

fact sheet

Six facts to know cold before the practice test

Passing score
70% on each section. Core has 25 questions; each Type section has 25.
Venting ban
Knowingly venting refrigerant during service, repair, or disposal is illegal. Since 1992 for CFCs/HCFCs and 1995 for HFCs.
Three Rs
Recover = pull it out. Recycle = clean it on site. Reclaim = reprocess to AHRI 700 spec off site (the only refrigerant you can resell).
Cylinder fill limit
80% of water capacity, max. Liquid expands with heat; past that, cylinders rupture.
Pressure test gas
Dry nitrogen only — never oxygen, never compressed air. Both can cause ignition in a refrigerant circuit.
Certification is for life
EPA 608 doesn't expire. Pass it once, you're done.
