Core

foundation · everyone takes it

Ozone depletion & the atmosphere

The stratospheric ozone layer absorbs harmful UV-B radiation. Chlorine atoms released from CFCs and HCFCs destroy ozone molecules — a single chlorine atom can destroy thousands of ozone molecules before being deactivated.

Two measures of refrigerant environmental impact:

  • ODP (Ozone Depletion Potential) — R-11 is the baseline at 1.0.
  • GWP (Global Warming Potential) — CO₂ is the baseline at 1.0.
Likely question: HFCs have zero ODP (no chlorine) but high GWP. HFOs have near-zero of both.

Safety groups (ASHRAE Standard 34)

Two letters: toxicity (A = low, B = higher) and flammability (1 = none, 2L = mild, 2 = moderate, 3 = high).

  • A1 — low toxicity, no flame propagation (R-22, R-134a, R-410A).
  • A2L — mildly flammable, lower toxicity (R-32, R-1234yf).
  • B1 — higher toxicity, no flame propagation (R-123).

The venting prohibition

Since July 1, 1992 (CFC/HCFC) and November 15, 1995 (HFC/other substitutes), knowingly releasing refrigerant during service, maintenance, repair, or disposal is prohibited.

Exceptions (de minimis):

  • Small releases associated with good-faith recovery.
  • Releases from purging hoses or connections.
  • Mixtures of refrigerant and nitrogen used for leak testing.

Three Rs: Recover, Recycle, Reclaim

ProcessWhereDefinition
RecoverOn-siteRemove refrigerant from a system and store it in an external container — no cleaning or testing.

ReclaimOff-siteReprocess refrigerant to meet AHRI Standard 700 (new-product spec). Only reclaimed refrigerant may be resold.

Evacuation (recovery) requirements

For equipment manufactured on or after November 15, 1993, you must evacuate to specific vacuum levels unless the unit is leaking and cannot reach them.

  • Small appliances (Type I): 90% recovery efficiency (80% if compressor is non-operational) — or achieve 4 in. Hg vacuum.
  • Type II high-pressure (<200 lbs): 0 psig.
  • Type II high-pressure (≥200 lbs): 10 in. Hg vacuum.
  • Type III low-pressure: 25 mm Hg absolute pressure (29 in. Hg vacuum).
Likely question: If a system is leaking and can't hold the required vacuum, you only have to recover to 0 psig before opening it.

Leak repair requirements

For appliances with 50 lbs or more of refrigerant, a leak exceeding the applicable annual leak rate must be repaired (or the appliance retrofitted/retired):

  • Commercial refrigeration — 20%/year (previously 35%).
  • Industrial process refrigeration — 30%/year.
  • Comfort cooling & all others — 10%/year.

Initial verification test after repair, then a follow-up verification test. Records must be kept.

Sales restriction

Only EPA-certified technicians may purchase regulated refrigerant. For HFCs, the sales restriction also applies (AIM Act rulemaking) in containers other than small cans under 2 lb with self-sealing valves for MVAC.

Refrigerant cylinders

  • Never fill above 80% of the cylinder's water capacity — liquid refrigerant expands with temperature.
  • Recovery cylinders have gray bodies with yellow tops (DOT 4BA or 4BW) and must be hydrostatically tested every 5 years.
  • Disposable cylinders may not be refilled — it's a DOT violation.
  • Ship under DOT as "Refrigerant gas, n.o.s., 2.2."

Safety

  • Refrigerants are heavier than air — displace oxygen in low areas. Watch for asphyxiation.
  • At open flame, refrigerant decomposes into phosgene, hydrofluoric acid, hydrochloric acid — highly toxic.
  • Never use oxygen or compressed air to pressure-test a system with refrigerant — use dry nitrogen.
  • Wear safety glasses and butyl-lined gloves. Liquid refrigerant on skin causes frostbite.

Recovery equipment

Equipment manufactured on or after November 15, 1993 must be EPA-certified to meet ARI 740 standards. Techs must use certified equipment matched to the refrigerant type.

Type I — Small Appliances

small & sealed · ≤5 lb charge

What qualifies

Fully manufactured, hermetically sealed, factory-charged systems containing 5 lbs or less of refrigerant:

  • Household refrigerators and freezers
  • Window A/C units and packaged terminal A/C
  • Vending machines, water coolers, dehumidifiers
  • Small heat pumps (under the weight threshold)

Recovery requirements

  • If using self-contained (active) recovery: achieve 90% of refrigerant (80% if compressor inoperative) or 4 in. Hg vacuum.
  • If using system-dependent (passive) recovery: 4 in. Hg after two separate access points.
  • System-dependent recovery is only allowed for small appliances.

Access methods

  • Saddle valves (piercing-type) are commonly installed on the process tube for recovery access.
  • After service, a process stub must be pinched off and brazed shut — not left with the saddle valve.

Disposal

The final person in the disposal chain is responsible for ensuring refrigerant is recovered before the appliance is scrapped. A signed statement that recovery was performed may be accepted from the previous party.

Type II — High-Pressure

high pressure · the everyday cert

What qualifies

Equipment that uses a refrigerant with a boiling point between -50 °C and 10 °C at atmospheric pressure — high-pressure refrigerants like R-22, R-410A, R-404A, R-134a, R-407C. Examples:

  • Residential central A/C and heat pumps
  • Supermarket refrigeration racks
  • Commercial ice machines
  • Reach-in coolers and freezers over 5 lbs

Very-high-pressure (R-410A, R-13, R-503) is grouped with Type II on the exam.

Leak detection

  • Electronic leak detectors — fast, but false-trip near cleaners/solvents.
  • Ultrasonic detectors — catch leaks by sound; helpful in noisy plant rooms only if tuned.
  • Soap bubbles — cheap, reliable, won't find small leaks.
  • Halide torch — old, detects Cl-based refrigerants only (not HFCs).
  • Fluorescent dye + UV light — persistent, good for intermittent leaks.
  • Standing pressure test with dry nitrogen (never O₂) & a small trace of refrigerant.

Dehydration (evacuation)

After repair and before recharging, evacuate to remove moisture and non-condensables. 500 microns is the typical field target. A deep vacuum of 500 microns held for 10 minutes with the pump isolated indicates the system is dry and tight.

Retrofits

  • R-22 → R-407C or R-422 series for existing equipment (mineral oil → POE usually required).
  • Never mix refrigerants of different types. Never top off a blended refrigerant with vapor — charge as liquid to preserve the blend ratio (critical for R-410A, R-407C).
  • Zeotropic blends (400 series) have temperature glide — always charge as liquid.
  • Azeotropic blends (500 series) behave like a single refrigerant — no glide.

Type III — Low-Pressure

low pressure · vacuum-side chillers

What qualifies

Equipment using refrigerant with a boiling point above 10 °C at atmospheric pressure. Examples:

  • R-11, R-113, R-123
  • Large centrifugal chillers

Low-pressure systems operate in a vacuum on the low side — any leak pulls air and moisture in, not refrigerant out.

Recovery target

25 mm Hg absolute (≈ 29 in. Hg vacuum).

Purge units

Because low-pressure systems operate under vacuum, air and moisture collect in the condenser. A purge unit removes non-condensables. Modern (high-efficiency) purge units lose less than 0.1 lb of refrigerant per lb of air purged.

Water box & pressure

  • To speed recovery, warm the refrigerant to no more than 150 °F — heating blankets only, never an open flame.
  • A rupture disk set at 15 psig protects low-pressure vessels.
  • During recovery, watch the disk — pressure rising toward 15 psig means non-condensables are building up.

Leak testing low-pressure systems

Pressure-test at no more than 10 psig, using nitrogen (with a trace of refrigerant if needed). Over-pressurizing can rupture the vessel.

Test-day tips

strategy · independent of section

  • Read every choice. On 608 questions, two choices are often technically correct but one is more complete — pick the most specific.
  • Watch for "always," "never," and "only" — absolute qualifiers are usually wrong unless the rule is truly absolute (like the venting prohibition).
  • Know your pressures: 0 psig, 10 in. Hg, 25 mm Hg, 15 psig, 500 microns, 80% fill.
  • Know the dates: 1987 Montreal Protocol, 1992 CFC/HCFC venting ban, 1993 recovery equipment cert, 1995 HFC venting ban, 2020 R-22 production ban.
  • If you're guessing, eliminate anything that would release refrigerant to atmosphere — that answer is almost never right.

  • 1987 — Montreal Protocol signed.
  • 1990 — Clean Air Act adds Sections 608 & 609.
  • 7/1/1992 — CFC & HCFC venting ban.
  • 11/15/1993 — Recovery-equipment cert in force.
  • 11/14/1994 — Tech cert required to buy refrigerant.
  • 11/15/1995 — Venting ban extends to HFCs & substitutes.
  • 1/1/2010 — No new R-22 equipment.
  • 1/1/2020 — R-22 production & import banned.
  • 2020 — AIM Act signed (85 % HFC phasedown by 2036).
  • 1/1/2025 — New residential AC on A2L refrigerants.
  • 1/1/2026 — Leak threshold drops 50 lb → 15 lb.
  • 2036 — 85 % HFC phasedown target.

Common trick questions

  • Type I "open-book online" rule applies only to Type I. Core, II, III must be proctored.
  • "Recovery" ≠ "reclaim." Reclaim implies AHRI 700 purity.
  • 80 % cylinder fill is by water capacity, not refrigerant weight.
  • R-410A and R-454B are zeotropic blends — charge as liquid.
  • Low-pressure chillers can pull atmosphere IN when running under vacuum.
  • A2L is mildly flammable (low burning velocity), not the same as A3 hydrocarbons.
  • Nitrogen trace amounts are de minimis — only refrigerant releases are venting.
  • R-22 production is banned, but existing equipment can still be serviced with reclaimed R-22.
glossary

Glossary — 35 terms you'll see on the exam

Every one of these turns up in 40 CFR Part 82, ASHRAE 34, AHRI 700, or a real question stem. Each card leads with a plain-English definition and adds the technical detail underneath. Skim once; come back to anything that surprises you.

Law / regulation Industry standard Refrigerant science On the job
A1 / A2L / A3

A safety code for refrigerants. The letter is toxicity (A = low, B = higher). The number is flammability (1 = won't burn, 2L = burns slowly, 2 = burns, 3 = burns easily).

Examples: R-22 and R-410A are A1; R-32 and R-454B are A2L; propane (R-290) is A3. Defined in ASHRAE Standard 34.

AHRI 700

The purity spec recovered refrigerant must meet before it can be sold to a new owner.

Below this purity, it's "recycled" (same-owner reuse only) — not "reclaimed."

AIM Act

The 2020 U.S. law phasing HFCs down 85 % by 2036.

Full name: American Innovation and Manufacturing Act. The reason R-410A is being retired.

ARI 740

The performance test recovery and recycling equipment must pass to be EPA-certified.

A machine labeled "ARI 740 certified" has been tested for recovery efficiency on the refrigerants it claims to handle.

ASHRAE 15

The standard that says how big a room must be — and what alarms it needs — for a given refrigerant charge.

Especially important for A2L and B-class refrigerants in occupied spaces.

ASHRAE 34

The standard that names refrigerants ("R-22," "R-410A"…) and assigns the A1 / A2L / B1 safety groups.

Azeotrope

A blend that boils and condenses at a single temperature — behaves like one refrigerant.

Can be charged as vapor or liquid. R-502 is the classic example.

CFC

Chlorofluorocarbon — the first generation of refrigerants. High ozone damage.

R-11, R-12. U.S. production banned January 1, 1996.

Compressor process tube

The little tube on a small appliance you charge or recover refrigerant through.

After Type I recovery, you pinch and solder it shut — don't leave a saddle valve hanging.

Deep vacuum

Pulling pressure down below 1000 microns to dry out a system.

608 target: 500 microns held with the pump isolated.

DOT 4BA / 4BW

U.S. Dept. of Transportation cylinder ratings — the marking that means "OK to ship."

4BA = steel, 4BW = welded steel. Recovery cylinders need one of these and a hydro retest every 5 years (49 CFR 173).

GWP

Global Warming Potential — how much heat a refrigerant traps compared to CO₂ (which equals 1).

R-410A ≈ 2088, R-32 ≈ 675, R-454B ≈ 466, R-1234yf < 1. The AIM Act phasedown is weighted by GWP.

HCFC

Hydrochlorofluorocarbon. Less ozone damage than CFC, but not zero.

R-22, R-123. R-22 production banned January 1, 2020.

HFC

Hydrofluorocarbon. Zero ozone damage — but high global warming potential.

R-134a, R-404A, R-410A, R-32. Now being phased down under the AIM Act.

HFO

Hydrofluoroolefin. Almost zero on both ozone and global warming — the new generation.

R-1234yf, R-1234ze. Often blended with HFCs to make A2L mixtures like R-454B.

IEC 60335-2-40

The international rule that sets how much A2L refrigerant you're allowed in a room of a given size.

How R-32 and R-454B charge limits are decided.

Low-loss fitting

A hose connector that seals itself when you disconnect — so you don't lose a puff of refrigerant every time.

Required by 40 CFR 82.158 on recovery and service equipment.

MACS / MVAC

Motor Vehicle Air Conditioning. Cars are regulated under Section 609 — not 608.

Different cert for the same skill set; you need 609 to work on car A/C.

Micron

A unit for very low pressures: 1 micron = 1/1000 of a millimeter of mercury.

500 µ is the dehydration target. Anything above 1000 µ means you're not dry.

Montreal Protocol

The 1987 international treaty that started phasing out CFCs and HCFCs.

The reason ozone-depleting refrigerants got scheduled out of production.

Near-azeotrope

A blend with such a small temperature glide that it acts almost like a pure refrigerant.

R-410A is near-azeotropic — but you still charge it as a liquid to keep the 50/50 ratio right.

ODP

Ozone Depletion Potential — how much ozone damage a refrigerant does, with R-11 set to 1.0.

R-22 ≈ 0.055. All HFCs and HFOs are 0.

POE oil

Polyol ester lubricant — the oil that goes with HFC systems.

R-134a, R-410A, R-404A, R-32, R-454B all use it. Soaks up water fast, so keep the jug capped.

Purge unit

The device on a low-pressure chiller that pulls air and moisture out of the condenser.

Limited to 0.1 lb of refrigerant lost per lb of air purged (40 CFR 82.157).

Reclaim

Reprocess refrigerant at a certified facility back to AHRI 700 purity.

Only reclaimed refrigerant can be sold to a new owner.

Recover

Pull refrigerant out of a system into an external container — no cleaning required.

Always the first step, before any service or disposal.

Recycle

Clean recovered refrigerant on site (oil separator + filter-drier).

Allowed for the same owner's equipment only.

Retrofit

Switching a system from one refrigerant to another.

Usually needs a new label, compatible oil, and sometimes new gaskets. You can't drop A2L into equipment built for A1.

SNAP

EPA's "Significant New Alternatives Policy" — the program that decides which refrigerants are OK in which equipment.

SNAP rules drove the A2L approvals.

Subcool

How far below saturation the liquid line is sitting. Used to grade charge on TXV systems.

Measured = liquid-line temp; target = saturation at liquid-line pressure minus the manufacturer's spec.

Superheat

How far above saturation the suction line is sitting. Used to grade charge on fixed-orifice systems.

Measured = suction-line temp minus saturated suction temp.

Ternary blend

A three-component refrigerant blend.

R-407C is R-32/R-125/R-134a. Usually zeotropic with a noticeable glide.

Venting prohibition

It's illegal to knowingly release refrigerant during service, repair, or disposal.

40 CFR 82.154(a). CFCs/HCFCs since 1992; HFCs and substitutes since 1995.

Working loss / de minimis

Tiny unavoidable releases (like a hose disconnect with low-loss fittings) don't count as "venting."

The law targets knowing release, not trace amounts you couldn't realistically capture.

Zeotrope

A blend whose components boil at different temperatures — it has a temperature glide.

Must be charged as a liquid. R-407C and R-404A are zeotropic.

Where this material comes from

  • 40 CFR Part 82, Subpart F — regulatory text for venting, recovery, leak-repair thresholds, sales restriction, and technician certification.
  • ASHRAE Standard 34 — refrigerant designation and A/B toxicity + 1/2L/2/3 flammability classifications.
  • AHRI Standard 700 — the reclaimed-refrigerant purity spec that defines "reclaim" vs "recycle."
  • ARI 740 — performance spec for EPA-certified recovery and recycling equipment.
  • 49 CFR 173 — DOT rules covering the 80% fill limit, 5-year retest, and cylinder marking.
  • Montreal Protocol (1987) and the Clean Air Act (Sections 608 & 609) for the legal framework.
Always confirm against the current EPA Section 608 regulations.
